Handball Fouls



When participating in sports, understanding the rules is essential to avoid penalties, suspensions, and disqualification. In this article, we'll explain what constitutes a foul in handball, helping you navigate the game with confidence.

In a game of handball, a foul is committed when there's any unnecessary physical behavior. Additionally, goalkeepers using their hands outside of their designated goal zone also commit a foul. The severity of the foul determines the subsequent actions by the referee, which could range from a warning to a 2-minute suspension or even dismissal from the game.

Fouls like pushing or kicking the ball during open play result in a spot free-throw, with the game restarting from where the foul occurred. If the foul happens between the goal zone line and the 8-meter line, it results in a penalty shot.

Here's a list of actions that qualify as unnecessary aggressive physical behavior and are considered fouls in handball:

Barging, Charging, or Tackling:
Players cannot run directly into their opponents. Attacking players must attempt to sidestep defenders to create an opening.



Elbowing:
Using elbows to push opponents away while making space for a pass or a shot is not allowed.



Swiping:
While using arms to block ball movement on defense is legal, swiping at players when playing offense is a foul.



Hitting:
Punching, kicking, and any form of fighting result in automatic disqualification for the involved players.





Clinching or Holding:
Obstructing other players by holding onto their jersey, arms, legs, or hair is a foul, with the punishment determined by the degree of infringement as decided by the referee.





Snatching the Ball:
Ripping the ball out of an opponent's hands is against the rules.



Tripping:
Any form of tripping is prohibited in handball, and players must not obstruct the movements of others.



Pushing:
Pushing is considered aggressive physical behavior and can lead to a 2-minute suspension or even a red card.




In general, the rules of handball are designed to promote a non-violent sporting environment. While the game is competitive and can be physical, it's important to avoid unnecessary physical aggression towards opponents.





Consequences of Committing a Foul in Handball


When a foul occurs, the referee stops the game with a whistle and assesses whether the infringement warrants punishment. For common fouls like kicking the ball, the game resumes with a free throw. If a foul denies a clear goal-scoring opportunity, the referee may award a penalty shot. All fouls result in a loss of possession. If a player commits a foul deemed severe by the referee, they may receive a warning, a 2-minute suspension, or even disqualification from the game.





The Role of Yellow, Red, and Blue Cards


Handball officials use three types of cards to categorize fouls and infringements: yellow, red, and blue.





Yellow Cards


1. These cards serve as warnings, often issued for minor offenses.
2. Players and coaches can receive only one yellow card per match.
3. If a player or coach with a yellow card commits a foul, they must serve a 2-minute suspension.
4. A team can accumulate a maximum of 3 yellow cards in one game.





Red Cards


1. Red cards are given for severe fouls or violent outbursts, resulting in disqualification.
2. A referee can award a red card to a player or coach for any foul deemed to be outside the rules of handball..
3. If a player accumulates three 2-minute suspensions in one game, they receive a red card and must leave the game. Their team plays with one player down for two minutes before a substitute can enter..
4. Goalkeepers may also receive red cards for using their hands outside of their goal zones to prevent a goal-scoring opportunity or for tripping a player outside the goal zone..





Blue Cards


1. Blue cards are less common and are typically used in professional leagues and the Olympic Games.
2. They are reserved for serious misconduct, including fighting or verbally assaulting a referee.
3. A blue card may be shown to a player or coach who has already received a red card, indicating the need for multiple suspensions.
